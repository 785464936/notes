# 260607-ENG

Parenting tips obtained from [[phrases#hunter-gatherer|hunter-gathers]] in Africa may be the key to bringing up more [[content#contented|contented]] children, researchers have suggested. The idea is based on studies of communities such as the Kung of Botswana, where each child is cared for by many adults. Kung children as young as four will help to look after younger ones and “[[phrases#baby-wearing|baby-wearing]]”, in which [[infant#infant|infants]] are carried in [[sling#sling|slings]] , is considered the norm.

According to Dr Nikhil Chaudhary, an evolutionary [[anthropology#anthropology|anthropologist]] at Cambridge University, these practices, known as [[alloparent#alloparenting|alloparenting]] , could lead to less anxiety for children and parents... Dr Annie Swanepoel, a child [[psychiatry#psychiatrist|psychiatrist]] , believes that there are ways to incorporate them into western life. In Germany, one [[scheme#scheme|scheme]] has paired an old people‘s home with a nursery. The [[reside#resident|residents]] help to look after the children, an [[arrange#arrangement|arrangement]] [[akin#akin|akin]] to [[alloparent#alloparenting|alloparenting]] . Another measure could be encouraging friendships between children in different school years to mirror the [[vise#(un)supervised|unsupervised]] [[phrases#mixed-age|mixed-age]] [[play#playgroup|playgroups]] in [[phrases#hunter-gatherer|hunter-gatherers]] communities.

In a paper published in the Journal of Child [[psycho#psychology|Psychology]] and [[psychiatry#psychiatrist|psychiatry]] , researchers said that the western [[phrases#nuclear-family|nuclear family]] was a recent invention which broke with [[evolve#evolutionary|evolutionary]] history.This [[abrupt#abrupt|abrupt]] [[shift#shift|shift]] to an “[[intense#intensive|intensive]] [[mother#mother|mothering]] [[narrate#narrative|narrative]]”, which suggests that mothers should manage childcare alone, was likely to have been harmful. “Such [[narrate#narrative|narratives]] can lead to [[mater#maternal|maternal]] [[exhaust#exhaustion|exhaustion]] and have dangerous [[consequence#consequence|consequences]] ,” they wrote.

By [[contrast#contrast|contrast]] , in [[forage#forage|forage]] societies adults [[phrases#other than|other than]] the parents can provide almost half of a child’s [[care#care|care]] . One previous study looked at the [[efe#Efe|Efe]] people of the [[democracy#democratic|Democratic]] Republic of Congo. It found that [[infant#infant|infants]] had an average of 14 [[alloparent#alloparenting|alloparenting]] a day by the time they were 18 weeks old and were passed between [[care#caregiver|caregivers]] eight times an hour.

Chaudhary said that parents now had less childcare support from family and social networks than during most of humans’ evolutionary history, but introducing additional caregivers could reduce stress and [[mater#maternal|maternal]] [[press#depression|depression]] , which could have a “[[phrases#knock-on|knock-on]]” benefit to a child’s [[wellbeing#wellbeing|wellbeing]] . An [[infant#infant|infant]] born to a hunter-gatherer society could have more than ten caregivers — this [[contrast#contrast|contrasts]] [[stark#starkly|starkly]] to [[nurse#nursery|nursery]] settings in the UK where regulations [[phrases#call for|call for]] a ratio of one carer to four children aged two to three.

While hunter-gatherer children learnt from [[observe#observation|observation]] and [[limit#limitation|limitation]] in [[phrases#mixed-age|mixed-age]] playgroups, researchers said that western “[[instruct#instructive|instructive]] teaching”, where [[pupil#pupil|pupils]] are asked to sit still, may contribute to conditions such as attention [[deficit#deficit|deficit]] [[act#hyperactivity|hyperactivity]] disorder. Chaudhary said that Britain should explore the possibility that older siblings helping their parents "might also [[enhance#enhance|enhance]] their own social development."
